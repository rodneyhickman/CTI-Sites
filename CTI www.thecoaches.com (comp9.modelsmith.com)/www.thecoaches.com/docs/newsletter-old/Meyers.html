Start Designing Alliances Now

A lot of these articles are written by super-coaches with tons of experience. But I’m writing this article for those of us in Organization and Relationship Systems Coaching courses right now: to share some experiences and to encourage you to get out there and start experimenting with the work.

So far, I’ve taken Organization and Relationship Systems Coaching Fundamentals and plan to finish the rest of the program in the next few months. But groups aren’t new to me.  When I started my coaches training two years ago, it was preceded by seven years of studying improvisation & performance psychology for co-creative best practices. I’ve also been working the past 20 years in advertising, telling stories and moderating focus groups. So from the beginning, my interest as a coach has always been on what I call Group Creative Process: what and how people make what they make together. I also use theatre games for connecting people to their authenticity and spontaneity, and for building group skills.

Designing the Partnership Alliance is one of the first exercises of OSRC Fundamentals and can be summed up by the quiet inquiry: How do we want to be together? Our instructor, Marita, told a story of another student making a commitment to do 50 free Designing the Partnership Alliance (DPA) sessions before her next course. That challenge resonated with me. So on Monday, I sent an email to my community offering a free DPA and received about 25 responses in the first two weeks. Since then, I’ve been working as much as possible with romantic couples and small businesses (mostly still for one free session), and charging corporate clients for DPA work. Corporations really need this work. Everyone talks about the importance of good relationships, but where do we learn these skills? What part of our work day is generally budgeted for “relationship building?”

If I have any advice, it’s to jump right in. For most people, the experience of consciously and intentionally designing their alliance for an hour adds immediate value. Start with the idea that your client is the “third entity” (the relationship they are creating together rather than the two separate parts) to introduce them to systems thinking. Be curious: What does the relationship want? This perspective opens the conceptual space for the OSRC model to unfold. Here are the five basic questions I use for Designing the Partnership Alliance:

• What kind of atmosphere or culture do you want to create together?
• How would that feel? What would help you feel that?
• What would help this partnership flourish? Define “flourish.”
• What do you want your partner(s) to be able to count on you for?
• How do you want to be when someone drops the ball or things get difficult?

Take It to the Bank
While your clients are answering these questions to each other, listen to the form enveloping the content (or story) of what’s being said, to keep your focus on the relatedness rather than the partners.

As an example, during a day-long session with a marketing company and its manufacturing client, the issue was raised that deadlines were frequently missed. Rather than forge head-first into requirements, processes and accountability, we took a step back to discover what this meant to the team. People from the manufacturer shared about the company’s internal culture, about the difficulties of managing their bosses. Team members from the marketing company shared how missed deadlines and time crunches challenge their ability to staff properly and negatively impact their profitability. Team members were able to diffuse the tension around the issue by trusting enough to reveal themselves and to listen to their partners to find shared values. Then, we brainstormed ways the marketing company could help the manufacturers deal with the clients’ organization. And in the end, the team decided that since the timing pressures were not likely to improve, compensation agreements and staffing plans needed to be revisited. The trust work helped this group air, understand and address a recurring problem in a positive way that was based on shared values, not conflicting positions.

The DPA questions open the space for team members to state their best intentions up front, before problems arise, creating a bank of shared values, trust and goodwill. I remind the team that at moments of conflict, we have a choice of whether to assume the best intentions of our partners versus assuming the worst intentions. On stage, in improv, we call this “Making Your Partner Look Good.” As the trust deepens, encourage the team to create an image or flag or song or set of code words that capture the essence of their best intentions, as a reminder and as a touchstone to re-establish their trust in the future when the process gets confusing or difficult.

Building Trust
I position trust-building work as a way for a team to experience both vulnerability and safety at the same time. The vulnerability allows team members to learn about themselves and partners, and to more easily access their spontaneous and creative selves. Importantly, they are doing this together, not individually. The safety makes it easier for the team to get back to that creative and open place again. What if a team envisioned shared goodwill as a delicious co-created pie? What if members could have a metaphorical slice of this yummy pie at the weekly status meeting every Tuesday morning?

My trust-building work frequently involves playing games from improvisational theatre. Some games happen quietly, in pairs. Others are more active and involve the whole group. Improv started as a tool for learning, not for entertainment, and the games we play with corporate teams don’t have much in common with what you might see in a comedy club or on television.

Improv games are fun ways for people to deepen connections by telling stories of peak experiences or to discover things about their partners that are new. Games of any kind are great for adding variety to the energy and tempo of a day-long session. Lastly, improv in the office or on stage helps people connect better to their bodies and feelings. I also use these games to model and practice listening skills and build on ideas. In improvisational theatre and dance, we play a game called “Yes, and….” For an OSRC coach, you’ll see a big difference in the emotional field created when players respond to an idea with “Yes, and…” versus “Yes, but….”

Process, Benefits and Outcomes
In my proposals, I remind my clients that they spend tens of thousands of dollars pitching new business, yet have virtually no budgets for growing existing relationships. When they lose a client, they frequently don’t really know why. Like lots of people, they focus on work deliverables and have zero relationship strategy.

My clients are well-versed in the language of consumer benefit: What will we get out of this? It can be a bit tricky at first, learning how to position organizational coaching services. It’s easy to fall into the trap of talking about your work in terms of what you will do together in-session. But while clients may want to know something about the process, knowledge of how the process works is not necessary for the team to benefit. Additionally, your process is probably not a way to differentiate yourself from other coaches. 

I try to position my session designs (or “scores,” as in music) as deliberately incomplete: requiring collaboration and experience to make them whole. A good score for a day’s session might include an assessment tool and some games to deepen the learning, some trust building exercises, a DPA and some explorations of shared definitions of success.

Talking about session designs as “scores” also gives me an opportunity to emphasize that relationship coaching is not a function box. There’s not a prescribed outcome. Rather, my job is to help create an environment in which teams can design and develop the relationship they want. It’s sometimes a challenge to manage one’s own “consultant perspective” when clients start asking tough questions about how things will change for them based on the coaching. In response, try emphasizing your clients’ power, responsibility and resourcefulness in relationship.

DPA is an easy way to introduce systems work to teams and partnerships, and a great tool to experiment with and practice relationship coaching. Scout around in your niche; all around us, people are involved in making together that which is unavailable to them alone. That’s the reason people work in teams. Even a little systems knowledge goes a long way, since the change of perspective from a group of individuals to a system is so powerful. Since the time of Rene Descartes, the western world has been building a cult of individualism. But my reading of the global emotional field is that we’re ready for some new ways of relating to each other.
