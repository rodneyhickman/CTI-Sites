Getting Myself Out of the Way Around Money and the Selling of Coaching!

The “selling” of coaching and the money conversation are areas I have struggled with. Gremlins lurk in my preconscious mind saying things like, “She’ll say no when she hears my fees.” “If I raise my fees I’ll lose clients.” “No one will pay that!” “How dare I ask for that fee?” “I don’t know anybody who can pay me more.” (Can you hear the Whiner, Fearful and People Pleaser gremlins?) 

Another gremlin says things like, “Clients will come to me; I just have to show up!” (Note: This actually happens often, yet I still undervalue my services.) “I’m greedy wanting more money; I’m getting by.” “Salespeople are vultures; I don’t want to be a vulture.” “How can I ‘sell’ spiritual coaching? This is my ministry.” “How can I place a monetary value on something so intangible and sacred?” Can you hear the “Miss Spirituality 2006” gremlin? (Who else do you hear? I’m curious!)

These gremlins are generated by fear: of not getting enough, having enough, being enough, doing enough, having answers, being greedy and a vulture, losing a client or a prospect. Physically, my mouth gets dry, I clench my jaw and throat, my stomach knots, and I stop breathing. My head starts racing. I lose my connection with my client or prospect and my inner guidance, and go into fix-it, do-it-right mode. If I am unable to stop the spiral, I can be left feeling totally incompetent, providing lots of ammunition for the Judge, Jury and Hangman gremlins.

These reactions signal that this is definitely an edge for me, as it is for many of my clients! I have learned to note this fear when it crops up,  then check in with the client to determine if it’s theirs or mine. If it’s theirs, we explore it. If it’s mine, I breathe through it and mentally tuck it away to deal with it later. When I have not been able to do this, the session becomes stilted, the dance is disrupted and the fear morphs into my questioning my competency. In these moments, I have learned to either tell on myself or take a deep breath and get back “over there” with my client. Telling on myself often creates the opportunity for a deeper intimacy with my client, and I can do that without getting inappropriately personal: “I just got distracted for a second,” or “Something you just said got my head going. Would you mind giving me a second to get back over with you? Thanks!”  Depending on our relationship, the client may ask me to share more deeply. I’ve found that when I share my vulnerabilities, it gives clients permission to share theirs and our relationship deepens.

This money/selling edge has affected my ability to appropriately value the service, experience and expertise I bring to people’s lives. More importantly, this and my other edges affect how I relate to clients with similar issues, meaning that I potentially hold them in less-than-empowering positions. I am selling my clients short by not dealing with these issues in my own life. 

Through the self-management tools of mindfulness, detachment and deep listening, I have seen that these gremlins represent the obvious voices regarding my edge in this area. They are the “A” team. The real seduction of these conversations is the protection afforded from dealing with a deeper issue: what is it that I am really committed to? There is a distinction between wanting what I say I want and having what I say I want. If I say I want to be earning what I think I deserve, do I really mean it? Or am I uncomfortably content to stay stuck in the story of wanting to earn more, stewing in the continued elusiveness, familiarity, comfortable discomfort and stories that being in want creates?

I asked myself this hard question and saw that I have been more comfortable being “in want” than being committed to having what I want. Once I acknowledged, felt and let go of the ensuing hurt, sheepishness and activated Judge this awareness brought, I seriously began to inquire as to what it is I want. This inquiry raised my consciousness of how I use the word “want” in conversation and how it shows up in what my clients are saying, leading to some revelatory discussions and choices for them! 

If I truly am committed to having what I say I want, I need to take action. I started to take a hard look at my beliefs about money and what it means to sell my services. As soon as I began this inquiry, people who are wealthy and successful started to show up to support and challenge me. I am working with a coach who is challenging my beliefs about money, selling and partnership. I am learning facts about money from a woman who educates people about money, empowering them to build wealth. I pass this information on to my clients.

My focus has shifted to being more focused on the having not the wanting, on the process rather than the goal. I have restructured my fees and my approach with prospective clients. My coaching has reached a higher level of integrity, sharpness, connection and effectiveness, and my clients are making great progress in achieving their goals.

Self-management through gremlin awareness, honesty, taking a hard look at myself and changing some core beliefs, has led to insights that benefit my clients’ lives and my own. We all get to win! Continual self-management and growth for the sake of the whole is a priceless benefit of being a coach, repeatedly confirming for me that this is a sacred calling—and that’s not my gremlin!
