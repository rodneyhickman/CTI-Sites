All around me, I see people striving to make order and sense of the rapid clipping along of the world. Where is the balance? Where is the harmony? We are workaholics, we are work obsessed. We strive to find balance and time for renewal and fail. . . too much to DO! Work, I fear, is beginning to get a bad rap.

Don't get me wrong, I'm all for balance and resting and time for renewal. I know that it is important to take vacations and time out. I get it, and I have a confession to make: I LIKE to work. Like is actually too mild of a word. The truth is, I LOVE it. The satisfaction at the end of the day or the week of a job well done fills me up. It renews me. The joyful hum that weaves through people happy at their work. . .this brings tears to my eyes. The gritty, earthy REALNESS of sweat, be it physical or mental, as I put my shoulder to a daunting task. . . wow! What aliveness.

For me, the key is to be at choice. . . am I working the work or is the work working me? Have I lost sight of my purpose, become disconnected from what matters most? For the sake of what do I toil? Work for the sake of busy-ness is empty and meaningless. Work connected to a sense of purpose and vision is filled with joy and fulfillment. So I share this lovely poem (given to me last week by a dear friend) as a celebration... an honoring of work.

Wishing you joy,
Karen

To Be Of Use

The people I love the best
jump into work head first
without dallying in the shallows
and swim off with sure strokes almost out of sight.
They seem to become natives of that element,
the black, sleek heads of seals
bouncing like half submerged balls.

I love people who harness themselves,
as ox, to a heavy cart,
who pull like water buffalo, with massive patience,
who strain in the mud and the muck
to move things forward.

I want to be with people who submerge
in the task, who go into the fields to harvest
and work in a row and pass the bags along,
who are not parlor generals and field deserters
but move in a common rhythm
when the food must come in
or the fire be put out.

The work of the world is common as mud.
Botched, it smears the hands, crumbles to dust.
But the thing worth doing well done
has a shape that satisfies, clean and evident. . .
Greek amphoras for wine or oil,
Hopi vases that held corn, are put in museums
but you know they were made to be used.
The pitcher cries for water to carry
and a person for work that is real.

Marge Piercy
