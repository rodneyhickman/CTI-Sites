Education From the Storm

As an inspirational speaker/writer and coach, I always enjoy presenting “Run to Daylight: Transforming Potential into Prosperity.” It is the best when audience members are encouraged to believe in the law of circulation, and to believe that one good deed leads to another.

It’s a good reminder for us all as we try and balance our coaching businesses with the business of community service. How, when, where should we give of ourselves without the thought of financial compensation? When should we follow the immortal words of the great Ralph Waldo Emerson, who once said: “It’s one of the most beautiful compensations of life: No man or woman can sincerely try to help another, without helping themselves; serve and thou shall be served.”

A recent Colorado snowstorm reminded me of one possible answer to what can be a challenging question as we try to balance life:

“Hey Dad, that was a nice shot,” complimented my eight-year-old daughter.

A powerful system had descended upon Colorado’s Front Range. It was dumping several inches of heavy, wet snow on our lawns and roads. It was of major concern to us, especially because of two large and beautiful ash trees that dominate the scenery in front of our home.

These moisture-intensive storms that hit before the leaves fall wreak havoc with Colorado’s trees. Too often, they snap under the unexpected weight of the heavy and clingy precipitation. Rachel and I were throwing, respectively, a soccer ball and basketball into the trees to knock snow off the branches. My daughter was complimenting me for nailing one branch that was bent over near its breaking point before being freed from the weight of the slushy substance.

This “throw the ball into the trees” method is something I had never tried. In fact, I had just been introduced to the concept a few hours earlier, while driving home from work and spotting a neighbor doing the same with her trees.

“Dad,” squealed Rachel, her school sweatshirt soaked from the snow. “I finally hit that branch!”

As I chased down the basketball after an errant toss—hey, not ALL were accurate—another thought hit me like a frozen snowball: here we were, outside in the freezing weather, braving the elements, because we cared about the welfare of two ash trees? Is that crazy? Or could it be a reminder of another great lesson of life?

Without our attention, those trees were in serious danger of losing some major branches. We had to take action to prevent them from snapping. It made me think of relationships—our personal, professional and civic-minded relationships that so often feel the strain similar to a branch laboring beneath heavy snow. Are we so quick to respond in a way that will relieve the stress? Are we conscious of the burdens that may exist?

These defenseless trees were under assault from Mother Nature. It was quite easy to respond. It’s quite a different story isn’t it, when our gut feeling is telling us we’re betraying our own self-good, perhaps taking money from our pockets, by assisting others via community service, and not through compensation?

We’re taught in many different ways, languages and beliefs to be “generous and compassionate” toward others, but could that possibly mean we would be crossing the line of not being “generous and compassionate” to ourselves?

Bam. Another direct hit brings slushy snow tumbling off the branches. Whoops! Another errant throw sends the basketball across the street.

Another thought pops into my head. Perhaps the answer to knowing when to respond or not is common sense. A good example might be if an elderly neighbor, recently recovered from heart surgery, were trying to throw basketballs into the trees in a rescue attempt. They would need to ask for help.

Perhaps the same is true of community service. When your gut feeling is suggesting involvement would compromise your mental, physical or emotional health, it’s time to ask someone else to help. You don’t have to ignore the problem, but you understand it would be even more damaging, like the heart patient exerting too much effort, for you to provide assistance.

If that’s not the case, grab the ball and start firing away. The effort to help a healthy and productive community effort, or organization, is well worth getting a little soaked and damp because of the warmth that will emanate from within.

“Dad,” my daughter announced. “It’s 10:30. Time to go rent a movie.”

I was snapped from my other world. As I walked into the house and tossed all our damp clothes into the dryer, I was hit with a strong sense of gratitude—for the woman who taught me the ball trick, for my daughter’s help, for the reminder to one of life’s great lessons: care for one another.

On this day, it was a group of trees that needed help. Another day, again in the words of Emerson: something or someone will “vibrate that iron string” within you.

Listen to it, that’s where you should serve.

That revelation led me to speak and coach inmates within the Denver County detention facility. The recidivist rate is alarmingly high within those walls. Too many men and women are returning to life behind bars instead of becoming functioning members of our society.

The costs are enormous in all areas: familial, societal and economic.

Victory Productions through its inspirational presentations, publications and products is going to try to make a difference, to try to be a spark that might encourage “change” in that area. Isn’t that what coaching is all about, to foster change?

Where could your time, talents and treasures perhaps be a spark of hope?

What is rooted in “one good deed leads to another” could someday lead to publicity of your good works growing beyond your local community to more state, regional and national organizations willing to compensate for your coaching expertise.

Think about it: what starts with you giving of your time, talents and treasures can circulate and result in rewards that stretch far beyond the financial.
