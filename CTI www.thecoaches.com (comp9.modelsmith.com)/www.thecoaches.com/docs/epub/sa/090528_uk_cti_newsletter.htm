The Five Contexts of Co-Active Coaching: Listening
By Mark Lister. CPCC


Human beings aren't solitary creatures. We thrive on our connections with one another, and those connections are what communication’s about. It's through communication that everything we achieve gets done, from opening a restaurant to flying to the moon to raising children. But while talk is easy, without the ability to listen keenly, communication goes nowhere.

Before coaching, if I'm honest, listening wasn't something I was very good at. I generally had plenty to say for myself, and a belief that everyone else was bound to be interested in my wisdom. This doesn't work when you're coaching, however, and so I've had to learn some lessons about listening. But the thing that's struck me most of all is that it's incredible what you can hear when you really listen.

Uniquely, in Co-Active Coaching, we talk about three levels of listening. What distinguishes them is where you focus as you listen to the person you're with.

Level One listening is listening primarily to yourself, or your own thoughts or agenda. You could be focusing on any number of things. Maybe you're thinking what to say next in the conversation, and so only half-hearing what the other person's saying. Maybe you're wondering what to have for lunch, or if you left the gas on. The key thing is that in Level One listening, you're not really fully hearing the other person.

In Level Two listening you are intensely focused on what the other person is saying. Nothing's distracting you. Thoughts about the past or the future don't intrude. Even your own ideas don't get in the way of you hearing the other person.

Level Three listening is also completely directed towards the other person, but it has a wider focus. You hear more than just the words they're saying. You pick up on all sorts of other things - body language, the inflections and tone of their voice, their pauses and hesitations. It's like you can hear sound effects in their mind - the clink of a penny dropping, the thud as they hit a wall. You can feel them straining to avoid something, or pulling towards something - and you have a sense of what that might be.

It's in Levels Two and Three that Co-Active Coaching take place. This is where you really let yourself be on the receiving end of someone else and their agenda.

Here's a simple experiment you can do to demonstrate the difference between listening Levels One, Two and Three.

  1. Get together with somebody else.
  2. Take a moment to think of something you really love - a particular interest or hobby perhaps, or a favourite book or film.
  3. Take it in turns to talk about the thing you love - for, say, 2 minutes. When you're listening, don't ignore the person, but don't let them hook you in either. Think about what you want to say on this subject, or let random thoughts distract you. When you're talking - and when you're listening too - notice what's like.
  4. Do it again, but this time, when you're listening, really focus. Be fascinated. Be alert to anything that makes you think "Wow!" or want to know more, however small or large.
  5. Do it a third time. When you're listening this time, try to be aware of what this feels like to the other person. Feel their energy. Notice the glint in their eye, the animation in their body language. Open up all your senses and be receptive to what the other person’s opening up to show you.

This is an experiment you can repeat, varying and playing with it as you go. Try choosing different topics - things you hate, things of utter indifference. If you're feeling really ambitious, try really taking on Level Three listening by doing the exercise without talking - just use grunting, body language, whatever you like as long as you don't talk. See what you can hear then!

Profile a successful coach
Susan Moss, CPCC, PCC

Tell us a bit about yourself, including what you did before you were a coach and what has brought you to coaching.
I have always been fascinated by human development.  I’m endlessly curious and driven to learn, so have always been looking for new opportunities, to apply what I knew to have more impact in the world.  So, after I graduated with a degree in psychology and a short career in human resources with a large retail business, I found my calling as a primary school teacher.

I worked my way up and became a headmistress of a large faith school in North London. It had its challenges, but not as many as the multi cultural school, I subsequently became headmistress of. It was there that my work began to become more coach like -- although I didn’t know it at the time.

Many of the children I was working with had been written off as failures, but I did a lot of values work with my team, to envision the kind of impact we wanted to have on these children. We agreed that we wanted to create a safe and loving environment in which they could relax and learn without being in fear. This had a massive impact on them academically, but also I believe it made them happier. It also made the teachers happier too, because I supported them in doing their job in a way they felt was best for them.

What is the biggest personal breakthrough that you've had because of coaching?
When I took the Co-Active Leadership Program about nine years ago, I felt like I had been steam cleaned on the inside! During the program I discovered my unique voice, and learned to trust that what it told me was right. Then, about a year after the course ended, I realised just how unhappy I was in my marriage. Because of my training I was able to tell the truth about what I wanted from my marriage, while also accepting that even if I didn't get it, and the marriage ended, I would still be okay, or creative, resourceful and whole as Co-Active coaches call it. That was so powerful, because it allowed me to transform a situation in my life, that might have become stagnant for so much longer.

You've developed a niche over the years coaching teenagers, how has this deepened your perspective on the Co-active Coaching model?
Teenagers, when they are empowered to find their own answers and solve their own problems, make changes very quickly, and this reinforces that the same thing is possible with anyone, of any age, in any situation.

Tell us what it was like as an ex headteacher, to go back into a school setting as a coach?
My antenna/sensitivity to the underlying communication problems, and lack of team work, and entrenched positions was now much sharper, and that was quite unsettling at first. I was sharply aware of the lack of co-activity in schools -- the ability to trust others and create understanding - and this was upsetting for me.

And yet what is what drives me to carry out this work and continue to support schools to change, is the magic that happens when you support children and teachers, in having a more fulfilling education and worklife, by doing what's truly in their hearts.

What advice would you give to coaches looking to develop their niche in the public sector?
Recognise the value that Co-Active Coaching truly offers. Especially that it's economical, and can have a big impact on people in a relatively short number of sessions.

Get the professional qualification, such as a CPCC and display your letters on everything -- it is respected in the education business!

In education -- make sure you are CRB checked (criminal records bureau in the UK) so that you can work in a school. Offer some voluntary sessions if you can and use what results as testimonials, and especially consider working with others in the same field.

Above all, follow the funding. Invariably, there are already organisations set up to provide training, to the public sector, and they are more likely to get funding than a start-up. So it may be easier therefore to align yourself with these organisations than try and apply for the funding yourself.

In building a successful business, what's your top marketing tip?
Don't be a well kept secret!

Of course, the more obvious marketing channels such as networking at conferences, local business groups, writing articles for local press, and doing radio interviews, are valuable, but don't underestimate the value of public speaking.

Speaking is just coaching in public -- you have so many tools already from the CTI courses that you can use in a group setting. Offering free talks to any of the 1000s of groups, clubs and societies out there is a great way of getting your name established.

In the public sector, you may like to offer free sample sessions at national conferences, but instead call them 'coaching clinics'. This is a great way of getting larger projects -- as opposed to individual clients. I once won a £10K plus contract as a result of one of these clinics.

What's your greatest client success story?
What a question!

I must be too modest as this is hard to complete. A recent success was a young man of 17 who got his teachers to sit down and listen to him while he explained the way he learned best, while also asking them to help him by slowing down and reinforcing the learning points -- to which they agreed. He set the agenda, set the whole thing up without his parents, spoke from his heart -- and essentially reversed the relationship by asking for what he wanted. Brave -- huh!

What's your greatest client failure story and what did I learn from it?
I was coaching a team of six senior teachers at a secondary school, and they had a new head teacher who could see the reluctance amongst the staff to change. I coached everyone individually, and the results were very mixed. There was a great impact with 4 of them, but 2 were just so entrenched in their position, they wouldn't change.

What this highlighted for me in group coaching, including in the business sector, is the need to ensure that all participants are willing to engage -- and are not just showing up, because they have been told too. It's your responsibility as a coach to do your due diligence here, because when even just one member of the group is holding back, it can scupper your overall impact on the team.

If someone else is paying for the coaching, other than the client, be absolutely sure who is accountable to whom, and how confidentiality is going to be held and honoured.

This is an important part of my work with teenagers too. I make it clear with parents who pay for the sessions, before they decide to sign up, that I can't report back to them about the teenager, and that a way must be established in which the teenager will be able to feedback directly to their parents.

You are one of CTI UKs mentors, what do you enjoy about that role?
I love being able to use the Co-Active model to help people do the practical things they need to do to become powerful and successful coaches, like finding their niche, marketing, structuring events to publicise your work, and how to deal with difficult client problems, ethics.

The focus of the mentoring service is to help people get going again when they've got stuck, or get out of the blocks when they haven't even started. I'm always amazed at just quickly people breakthrough and re-ignite their passion for what they want to create in the world.

If you had 1 minute to advise someone considering a career in coaching, what would you tell them?
You will learn a set of skills that are totally portable, applicable in numerous life situations, personal and professional, to help people change their lives, and achieve fulfilment according to their values. This in itself is immensely fulfilling and energising work to do!

What is the one piece of advice about starting a career as a coach that you wish you had been given?
Keep really thorough client records, hours and testimonials for gaining professional awards such as PCC.

Also you MUST get professional indemnity insurance -- I use Towergate.

What do you like to do for fun?
The printable stuff is: go on a boat and cruise anywhere; theatre and movies; scuba and snorkelling.
