Top 10 List for Building Success in Coaching in Organizations
by Kendall Colman, MHSA, CPCC

Please join us monthly for our telecall for more strategies to help you be successful coaching in organizations.

  1. Back to basics. Take your Future Self out for lunch and visualize the kind of business, the clients and the income you want. Volumes of information exist about how to take yourself (or, hey, hire a coach) through the discovery of creating your ideal business. If you don’t know where you are going, any road will take you there.
  2. Be clear about your fees and value. Determining fees, especially in a market where “average rates” are so widespread, is not only a practical exercise (e.g., hourly rate, retainer, packages, annual salary), but also requires the use of intuition as well. Read some of the great books on the market describing different pricing scenarios. Good questions to ask yourself for this process include: What do I know now about the market? How do I know it? What are the real costs? Will it work for me, for the client? The possibilities: What else can I come up with? What other interesting ideas are there? What is a new way to do this? What is my hunch about these fees and my value? For more, listen to last month’s call on pricing at: 405-244-4000, box 606.

Home, Home on the Range
by Gail Barrie, CTI staff

Have you ever noticed how our full range of emotion gets such a bad rap? Seems like I’ve been noticing this my entire life.

From where I sit, this “bad rap” is a global phenomenon, not just a weird Western idiosyncrasy. It’s a phenomenon that drives who we are in boardrooms, in bedrooms, around the dinner table and in places of worship all over the world. Having worked extensively with my own emotions and with clients internationally, I see a consistent theme: an unconscious belief system that says: “Too much emotion is a bad thing…moderation or non-emotion is better.” No matter the culture, the underlying message is, “Rein in your emotions or suffer dire consequences.”

What slays me about this oversimplified LIE is that it applies to all emotions, not just a few. Don’t have or show too much anger…too much optimism…too much grief…too much joy, hatred, hope, despair, trust. Bottom line: DON’T FEEL TOO MUCH!

 Ask Judith

Dear Judith:

I have a very embarrassing problem. I am envious of a specific client. She’s in her early thirties, married to a wonderful man who adores her. She has a mid-level management job at a local corporation and seems to be on the fast track to success. I claimed this woman as my client because she is successful. A few weeks ago, she told me that she and her husband are pregnant with their first child. Somehow this wonderful news has brought up these ugly feelings in me.

Why should she get everything she wants and not me? I am angry and envious of her. I feel myself tighten up at this news. I have managed to hide my feelings from her, but I am ashamed of myself for feeling this way. I don’t want to talk about this with anyone that I know because they’ll think that I am not acting like a professional. Please don’t even print my initials. Thanks for any help that you can give me.
—Anonymous

Dear Anonymous:
Thank you for having the courage to admit your feelings out loud. Even though you remain anonymous, it takes strength to come out and talk about something of which you are ashamed. Asking for help in the face of a problem is not only good for you, it is also the mark of a professional.
