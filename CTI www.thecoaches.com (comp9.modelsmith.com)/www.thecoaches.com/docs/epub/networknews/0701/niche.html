Coaching Impacts Recovery Community

by Alida Schuyler, Haskell Hestand, Theresa Swift

Addiction costs Americans more than $100 billion a year in lost productivity, social disorder and excessive use of healthcare services. This affects everyone: health insurers and public health agencies see disproportionate use of emergency rooms, general medical and psychiatric services; employers pay for substance abuse treatment and need their employees to return to full functioning; and the criminal justice system wants less crime, incarceration and parole violations.

At home, families want to live without the worries, embarrassment, violence and financial concerns that go hand-in-hand with substance abuse. And addicts themselves have needs and wants. They want relief from discomfort caused by the direct effects of substance abuse, as well as improved personal and social functioning, resulting in fewer problems. Everyone wants the costs of addiction to come down, especially the cost of repeated treatment. Unfortunately, the majority of addicts relapse soon after treatment.   

Recovery Coaching Enhances Quality of Life and Prevents Relapse 
Recovery coaching is highly effective support when the addict comes home from treatment. Recovery coaching reduces stress by helping the addict and family build awareness and focus on true values and strengths. Co-Active coaching forwards the action with purpose and direction, and the addict addresses his or her problems quickly and effectively. Recovery coaching helps the family and addict get connected with 12-step or other local community support. As a result, stress does not build up, quality of life improves and vulnerability to relapse decreases. Here are illustrations of recovery coaching at work within the recovery community:

Lloyd
I cured a three-year cocaine addiction with a seven-year methamphetamine addiction, for which I then substituted a 120-pound weight gain. The disease of addiction manifested itself in many ways. I remember how lost I felt as I drifted over the years from treatment to therapy, to Narcotics Anonymous, to Overeaters Anonymous and finally to Alcoholics Anonymous. While I diligently worked the 12 steps in each program, I stopped using, drinking and compulsively overeating, but never seemed to move beyond program into the new life that I wanted. I began to think that my Higher Power had created me just to “recover,” over and over again.

Finally, with the guidance of a Co-Active coach, I’ve been able to move beyond thinking of myself only as a drug and food addict and into the wonderful life that was waiting for me on the other side! I see now that it wasn’t enough to surrender to my addictive substances. I needed to move beyond my past and grow toward my Future Self. And Co-Active coaching has enabled me to do just that.

Sheryl
I started drinking at the age of 11—from wine to whisky—and drank and drugged straight until I stopped by the grace of AA when I was 29. When I had 14 years of 12-step recovery (I now have 23 years), I came across coaching and hired a Co-Active coach. At that time, I was underearning and underemployed. I thought of myself primarily in terms of being an alcoholic. I lived one day at a time. My coach asked me to drop the story about being a drunk. She challenged me to quit focusing on my past and my limitations. My coach asked me what I wanted and how was I going to get it. She taught me to set goals and plan ahead. I quickly saw my life blossom because of coaching. I loved being held creative, resourceful and whole. I learned to plan my life and apply “one day at a time” to drinking rather than to my whole life.

Howard
I often wonder whether I’d have relapsed all those times if my early recovery had been supported by coaching. With the strength-based recovery coaching model, I would have been encouraged to explore relapse prevention techniques, physical health restoration, career resurrection and relationship growth…to construct and establish a new lifestyle that wasn’t dependent on alcohol and other drugs. A recovery coach had the potential to complement the work of counselors and sponsors, and to re-engage me with my life and the direction in which I wanted it to grow.

Ironically, I became an addiction counselor prior to becoming a coach. I worked with patients as they moved toward discharge from our treatment center, and I saw most of them flounder in early recovery and many of them readmit into treatment. Now, as a recovery coach, I’m able to contribute to a significant reduction in relapse, and help those in recovery move beyond self-identification as addicted individuals, into a new life of growth and joy!

Kayla
After a long recovery road, I found coaching and recognized it as the career I had long been searching for. I loved coaching from the get-go. I loved it because it was a fun way to help people. I could bring everything I knew, my whole self to it. I could coach clients about their beliefs and values, or their business and money habits. By coaching and being coached, I was growing faster than the people in my home group. I began to want alcoholics and other addicts to know about coaching. I decided to devote my life to growing recovery coaching into an estimable profession with a solid philosophical base—to reflect the best of coaching and chemical dependency.

How Can You Play? 
Niche marketing will evolve within recovery coaching along several predictable lines, including: a) type of addiction, such as overeating or undereating, nicotine addiction or Internet porn addiction; b) preferred method of recovery, e.g. Christian-based recovery coaching, alternatives to 12-step, harm reduction, etc.; c) expertise in common issues in recovery, such as relapse, co-dependency, dating and romantic relationships in recovery, financial recovery; d) coaching that is adapted to meet the specific needs of a particular ethnic or cultural group, such as Native Americans or Asians; e) coaching a particular stage of recovery, such as early recovery, relapse or long-term recovery.

Clearly, there are many ways to play in the “recovery sandbox!” There’s room for all to create and pioneer their own, unique recovery coaching career.
