Waiting for 2007

by Aysegul Yilmaz, life coach

Another year has ended! I am sure you are hearing this sentence a lot these days. As many of us do at this time every year, I start to review the previous year’s balance sheet and start questioning myself. What did I do? What didn’t I do? Why didn’t I do it? If I really wanted to, what would I do? Start making new resolutions for the coming year.

Since the beginning of December, I am in this mood. But this year, I have a different interest for New Year’s resolutions because I think it is a good way to start a coaching relationship as well. Therefore, I decided to do a little search; I wrote “new year resolutions” in the Google search box and was amazed by the result. This topic is extremely popular! Another surprising discovery for me are the New Year’s resolutions made all around the world that are very similar:

  1. Lose weight and get in better physical shape.
  2. Stick to a budget.
  3. Reduce debt.
  4. Enjoy more quality time with family and friends.
  5. Find soul mate.
  6. Quit smoking.
  7. Find better work.
  8. Learn something new.
  9. Volunteer and help others.
  10. Get organized.

Did you find anything familiar?

It is good to define targets for the New Year. Making a decision for a new start and thinking of improving life quality is a good feeling and brings excitement to our life. To achieve a resolution, focus, accountability, persistence and personal integrity are required. Focusing on each goal separately, having a plan, having support from a friend/mentor/life coach, being persistent and determined—all will increase the level of success.

We all make New Year's resolutions, but how many of us are keeping them? In their article “If at First You Don’t Succeed,” Polivy and Herman cite statistics that:

  • A full 25% of New Year’s resolutions will be abandoned in the first 15 weeks.
  • The average number of New Year’s resolutions made is 10.
  • Those who manage to make a resolution that lasts for six months or more have often tried five or six times before finally succeeding.
  • Many New Year’s resolutions are for health-related goals.

This year, what would you do differently to make your resolutions work? You may start working on a plan. To have a goal without a plan will not take you anywhere. With a plan you can define what to do next and what other steps you need to complete to reach your goal.

If you don’t want to forget your resolutions by the end of February, you had better start planning now! Writing down the resolutions and reviewing them from time to time will show how committed you are.

Let me remind you that flexibility (dancing in the moment!) is also very important while making resolutions. These are your resolutions and you have right to change or revise them along the way. And as long as your resolutions/goals/targets—however you want to call it—overlap with your values, the success rate will definitely increase.

When I started researching and writing this article, I was thinking of finding different ways to start a coaching relationship in 2007. But then I realized that, while working with our clients, we coaches tend to forget ourselves. We help them identify their values, potential, goals, life purpose and so many other things, but are we also doing this valuable work for ourself? Don’t you think that we deserve it?

Dear coaches, have a wonderful 2007!
