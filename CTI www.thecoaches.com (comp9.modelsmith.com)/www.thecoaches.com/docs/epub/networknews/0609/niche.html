Top 10 List for Building Success in Coaching in Organizations
by Kendall Colman, MHSA, CPCC

Please join us monthly for our telecall for more strategies to help you be successful coaching in organizations.

1. Back to basics. Take your Future Self out for lunch and visualize the kind of business, the clients and the income you want. Volumes of information exist about how to take yourself (or, hey, hire a coach) to take you through the discovery of creating your ideal business. If you don’t know where you are going, any road will take you there.

2. Be clear about your fees and value. Determining fees, especially in a market where “average rates” are so widespread, is not only a practical exercise (e.g. hourly rate, retainer, packages, annual salary), but also requires the use of intuition as well. Read some of the great books on the market describing different pricing scenarios. Good questions to ask yourself for this process include: What do I know now about the market? How do I know it? What are the real costs? Will it work for me, for the client? The possibilities: What else can I come up with? What other interesting ideas are there? What is a new way to do this? What is my hunch about these fees and my value? For more, listen to last month’s call on pricing at: 405-244-4000, box 606.

3. Build a sizable sales funnel. The best way to do this is by constantly building relationships with people. Whenever you are communicating with a prospect, provide something of value.  Have your “sales pipeline” full, as you don’t know when your clients will turnover. Build in a product funnel to support your sales efforts, for example: speaking engagements, ezines, teleclasses, mentoring programs. Some are free and some are paid, and everything always adding value. Question: What is something unique I provide to clients? What do I enjoy doing that will add more value to my clients? The idea is to have the prospect think, "If I received this much value in correspondence, how much would I receive if I actually hired this person?" 

4. Networking.  The power of networking is not in the quantity of the contacts, but in the quality of the contacts. Do research ahead of time. Learn who will likely attend the event. Introduce yourself without describing anything about your work and simply listen, ask questions. In the event you're asked what you do, practice providing very succinct responses. Immediately follow up, the next morning at the latest, deliver what you promised. I won’t go to another networking meeting until I have followed up sufficiently with the one I just attended.

5. Don’t sell, coach. I’m amazed at how many of our selling conversations (remember enrollment?) can easily be turned into coaching conversations. In all selling situations, networking, meetings, etc., trust yourself and slightly hold back attention so you can focus on the intelligent information available to you in the Level 3.

6. Design the alliance, and often: Make sure you do a scope of work, or memorandum of understanding when signing on for any coaching project within an organization. As people are having more exposure with a business or executive coach, it is important to be professional and get agreements vocalized and on paper. Also, design regular feedback with your clients about how you experience them, their company or their process. Remember that the number one derailer for leaders and their teams is the inability to ask, give and receive feedback. It is important to model for them how to do this.

8.  Constantly educate yourself about what is going on in the business market. Read one business book a month. I just read Nuts! which was a terrific read about fulfillment in action at Southwest Airlines. And make a daily practice to read the local business journal. You don't have to read it cover-to-cover, but simply peruse the headlines so you have an idea of what’s happening, who’s who, etc. Also send congratulation letters to those “on the move.” People in transition use coaches.

9. Refine your materials. Know the balance between providing structure to your coaching offering and dancing in the moment. Coaching in organizations is more and more about return on the investment, so make sure you confirm how the client and boss (or sponsor) will know the value of the coaching. A good question for the discovery session is: How will achievement of these goals be demonstrated?

10. Subcontract and take time off. Can’t learn Dreamweaver and Quickbooks, too? If something is not in your skill set, then subcontract. Allow for rest and reflection. Allow the natural process of ebb and flow of building your business to occur. Being in start up mode can be exhausting over long
