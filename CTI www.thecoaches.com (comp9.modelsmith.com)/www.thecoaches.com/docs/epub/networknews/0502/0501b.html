The Coaching Salon

I have been playing with a forum I created, The Coaching Salon, over the last six months. Turns out it's not a new name. Someone under the auspices of Coachville has been using that name for several years, with a similar bent on helping others learn about coaching through a shared experience of observing live coaching. Even the act of watching someone else demonstrate a discipline is not new. Think cooking shows, home remodeling shows and black belt demonstrations at the local karate studio. What is new is the way in which The Coaching Salon transforms the participants as well as the observers. Here's how it works:

  1. A volunteer from the audience is coached for 12 minutes by a professional coach-someone who is trained as a coach.
  2. There is at least one other person observing the coaching and preferably more, including other professional coaches.
  3. Feedback follows each coaching. The volunteer client gives the first feedback and then the audience is invited to provide feedback.
  4. This cycle of coaching with feedback is repeated twice, each time with a different volunteer and different coach.

Sometimes, it's not clear what you have on your hands until someone else tries out your idea. A friend tried out The Coaching Salon in another part of the country. She did it with another coaching colleague. In the process of accommodating her colleague, this friend played and stretched the form of The Coaching Salon-from 12 to 15 minutes, coaching one-on-one instead of in front of others, guaranteeing confidentiality where it could not be guaranteed. And of course without observers, there could be no feedback. I'm fortunate to have this friend. She taught me what was important about The Coaching Salon, those parts that are non-negotiable, by inadvertently omitting the parts that give the Salon its uniqueness.

Five Conversations on the Frontiers of Leadership
David Whyte

Leadership gets a great deal of emphasis these days, though as a poet I often ask myself if there is a better word, or a better avenue of enquiry than putting all of our eggs in that one basket of exploration. Leadership dominates our conversations in organizations-large, small, entrepreneurial, or academic. I am often asked: "What is the secret of leadership?" as if it were a static piece of information rather than a dynamic relationship between an individual and the world. Some believe that if you simply adopt certain traits-being decisive, taking bold risks, or articulating a clear and compelling vision of the future, you will have the very stuff of leadership. But do any of these commonly observed traits make up that magic ingredient that invites others to join or follow an individual? It is my opinion that they are not enough by themselves. They are isolated diagnostic characteristics that a given leader may have, but to my mind they are not the heart of the matter.

The Art of Conversation
At the very heart of leadership-indeed, at its very soul-is the art of conversation, the ability to create a dialogue that others will willingly join. Any good conversation extends an implicit invitation for those who join it to meet at some as yet indefinable frontier, and through that joining to be part of the process of defining that new frontier. The best conversations make clear distinctions between what has gone before and what is now possible. They give the sense that we are part of something that is enlarging us or our organization rather than confining us.

Dear Judith:
I have a client who answers me with "I don't know," whenever I ask her a powerful question. I don't know what to do.
W. A.

Dear W.A.
Wow, I don't know either! Don't worry, I'm just kidding…. Actually, not knowing is really a great place to be. When you don't know, there are tons of opportunities to practice curiosity, beginner's mind and creating in the moment.

Here are a few ideas that you can try:

  • Get curious about the part of your client who doesn't know the answer. Invite that part of her to have a conversation with you.
  • What DOES your client know? She's got to know something.
  • Have your client pretend that she does know, and have her make up several answers in the moment. It doesn't matter whether she thinks her responses are right or not. We make stuff up all the time including the fact that we don't know. Be playful about it and see if anything rings true for her.
  • Let your client suggest her own questions that might work for her.
  • Just to be creative, ask her gremlin to answer the question and see what comes up. You might find a powerful answer here.

    • One other notion to consider is that sometimes people immediately answer, "I don't know," because they haven't yet taken the time to explore the question. For some people, it's like saying, "Um," or "Uh." It's a chance to pause and buy time to consider the question. The next time your client says, "I don't know," let her know that she can take some time to consider your question. If it's a powerful question, it deserves some good attention on her part. Be patient and wait. Trust that she is naturally creative, resourceful and whole, and is capable of coming up with an answer that works for her.

    January 2005 Network News

    I'd like to start off by saying, Thank You! to all of you who have been vigilant in managing your email accounts on the Network. Seems we're getting better and yet you can count on me to hold the bar high on this one. The trick, I assert, is in remembering all the places your email is connected to on any given day, especially when you're trying to tie down all those loose ends before you go away from your computer for a weekend getaway, business trip or extended vacation. Well, the Co-Active Network is one of those loose ends to tighten up before you leave. This seems to show up across the board and mostly with our corporate friends who enable "auto reply" on their email accounts. Please remember to go to "Manage Account" and select "no mail" or simply un-check all the boxes to the right of your communities in which you belong. Helpful Hint: Put a sticky note on your computer screen as a structure to remember that the Co-Active Network is impacted by your auto replies.

    You may have noticed that some of your discussions that promote services and opportunities have disappeared on the Main Community discussions list. Don't panic, I've moved them to the Promos thread. This is now the place to post all of your fantastic opportunities. If you're selling, you're promoting. Simple eh? This allows the receiver to choose. A much better impact indeed!

    Ever been one to look for a bridgeline for a specific event on the Network? Here's a couple of tips for you. First, go to the event description posted on the calendar. It may have been added to the description closer to the date of the event. In some cases, it's posted there at the time the event was created. Second, if it's quite a bit of time before the event, say five days or more, you may need to simply wait and see if you receive an email from the organizer closer to the date of the event. And third, this is usually saved for those desperate, last minute, "Oh my gosh, what's the number?" times: send an urgent email to the organizer of the community or post a discussion in the community of origin. Let me explain this last piece. Most all events except "Main Events" are generated in a particular Niche or Geographic community. Post your urgent plea to the community of origin. There is a link at the bottom of the event description to take you there. It looks like this: "This page is a pointer to this original event."
