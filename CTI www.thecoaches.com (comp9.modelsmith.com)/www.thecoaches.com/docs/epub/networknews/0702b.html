Hello Co-Activationists,

I’m curious. What’s the impact on you when I call you Co-Activationists?

I’d like to point not to the question, but to the curious piece. It’s in being curious that I can better assume (watch out for that too!) how it will land “over there” in your email box—or how it’s read online. Do you find yourself not responding to discussions because you make up that it’s not important or that what you have to share just won’t have that “splash” like others seem to? I smell a saboteur. Or, do you simply follow an urge to post, wanting to be seen and heard without really looking at the bigger picture? Are you ever curious how your contribution is received? I assert most of you are, and keep looking.

Yes, We Have an Agenda
Coaches Are Leading Social Change in Alberta, Canada
by Philip Durnford

Imagine coaching someone who has no access to a personal telephone, whose routine is navigated by meal times, opening and closing times and the probable length of a line-up. This is a person who may have a rarely viewed email address and has, at best, a planning horizon of two hours.

It is 30% likely that the client is addicted to drugs and/or alcohol and/or gambling or is in recovery from an addiction. It is also 30% possible that the client has been diagnosed with some form of mental disorder; commonly depression or bipolar disorder, occasionally ADD/ADHD or even schizophrenia.

As to the question of jail time, it is likely. Some abuse? Probably. On the plus side, it is 30% likely that the client is a graduate of at least a degree level education and 55% likely that the person works for a living. On the other hand, add that this person probably lives in a shared space, with others in a similar situation and has no personal space outside of his or her bed. It is even possible this person shares one room with 200 others and sleeps on a washable, plastic-covered foam mat each night.

The Caterpillar Story: A Visual Transformation
by Suzanne Ness

A few weeks ago, my 5-year-old was given a caterpillar at her preschool, along with instructions on how to care for the caterpillar. This was not unusual. Chloe, my 5-year-old, is fascinated with bugs of all kinds. She has brought home many creepy crawlies in different containers, hoping to raise them to adulthood and keep them as pets. She always willingly releases them when I explain that they will die in the container she caught them in. She has a regard for life that outweighs her desire to have bugs as pets.

We watched the caterpillar eat and move for a week. The instructions told us that it would shed five times as it grew, before it became a chrysalis. At that time, it would attach itself upside down to the lid of the container and remain that way for 7-10 days as it morphed into a butterfly. I, too, was curious, as I have never witnessed this phenomenon up close before. I also did not know what to expect.

 Network Support Wizardry

Hi Folks,

I thought I’d write on a couple of topics this month, one about double posts and the other about mindfulness in replying.

Double posting is one of those “ta da’s” created by the poster of a discussion. If you’ve crafted a discussion and sent it, then gone back because you noticed an error, corrected it and then resent it again (are you still following me?)…then a double post has been created. We’re all going to get it twice. That is, the members who have their “real time” notification set for that community. Those that are on “digest” mode won’t. Good luck!

Secondly, I’ve noticed a few “cheerleading” emails coming across the boards. Don’t get me wrong, I love celebrating and cheering people on. However, did you know that sometimes on the Main Community when you “reply all” from the links at the bottom of the discussion, your “Yay! You go and let us know!” email has hit more than 8,600 email in-boxes? Is that the impact you want to have? I make up that there’s a level of excitement and wanting to contribute that happens faster than taking a breath and getting curious as to what you want to say. My editorial in this issue addresses this. My request is to slow down and think about where your contribution(s) will end up. If you need help, contact me at networksupport@thecoaches.com.

Respectfully,

Andrew Gabel
networksupport@thecoaches.com

 Ask Judith

Dear Judith:

I have a client who makes it very hard for me to intrude and take charge. She speaks very quickly and hardly takes a breath between sentences. Do you have any good intrusion suggestions?
—V. B.

Dear V. B.,
I couldn’t help but immediately notice the way you phrased the first sentence of your question. “I have a client who makes it very hard for meto intrude and take charge.” Notice the difference between your perspective and this perspective: “I have a hard time intruding and taking charge with one of my clients.” Or possibly even this perspective: “I do not like to interrupt clients (or anyone for that matter) because I believe that it is rude and impolite.” In your version, you have given your power to the client rather than owning your part of the Co-Active coaching relationship. Do you see the difference between your statement and taking responsibility for your choice to not interrupt her?
