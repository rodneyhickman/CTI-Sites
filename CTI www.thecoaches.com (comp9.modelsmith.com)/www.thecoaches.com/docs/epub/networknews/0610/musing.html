An Inspirational Day of Co-Activity in Europe
by Jill McCullogh, Cambridgshire, UK

The European Co-Active Community Day—Harrington Hall Hotel, London—Friday, the 12th of May, 2006

“Where are my feet, where’s my butt?” This, I thought, gazing at the kind and cuddly form of Karen Kimsey-House, is not the leader of an international cult.

On a bright, sunny day, I found myself zinging with anticipation as I entered the somewhat cave-like, windowless innards of the hotel conference room. I had expected the huge throng that had been there last October, but this, it seemed, was to be a more intimate affair of maybe 40 in all.

We all bang on about the magic of Co-Activity, but it is that energy of passion, possibility and unconditional giving that fills the room and acts as a shot in the arm. That’s why I was there. Networking extensively, as many of us do, I’m sure, I never get the same vibration. Indeed, before I did my coach training with CTI, I wouldn’t have even known what to look for! I recently attended a meeting of the Professional Speakers Association; boy, did that feel different!

Kyle Newman, our host, brought us all into the meeting and our intentions were set for the day in true Co-Active style. Hushed, nodded comments of “the importance of bringing this work to the world” always have my “down-to-earth” saboteurs leaping up and down. Indeed, Karen mentioned that on a recent course, a member had queried CTI’s cult status. Yes, of course, if the whole world had a Co-Active coach, it would be a better place, but let us not hold our CTI leaders as gurus. They are only human, and we are not “less than,” merely fledgling in our brilliance. Karen certainly does not hold herself in that exalted, worshipful position. She is an expert, inspiring and generous with her knowledge, but real, and her method for getting present and connected at the top of the page says it all!

The morning was spent in the safe hands of Karen and Kyle, who danced playfully together giving us “Co-Active Magic and Workshop Wizardry” and, given Karen’s timeframe, gave a perfect example of how you can put together a workshop over a glass of wine if you know your stuff.

The session was BRILLIANT, the hand-outs an invaluable reminder…and it was a slow burn. It wasn’t until three days later that I woke up with a brilliant business idea full of inspiration, which had all been fed from that workshop.

The central position was to hold the big picture of the workshop, not to get bogged down in the details. Once you have established who you want to make a difference with, it is about satisfying their hunger. Underneath all that, the real core to the piece is the stake: What is the ultimate point of you running the workshop? What do you want them to be left with? “For the sake of what?” 

This was a piece of re-learning that resonated on so many levels for me and now underpins almost every meaningful conversation and business meeting I have in my life. As a big-picture person, I get dangerously distracted (and bored) by the detail. I have always set intentions of what to get out of a business meeting, but this is different. This is a way of giving focus to any workshop, indeed any meeting, in a powerful way. By the way, it’s also handy in handling sophisticated bed-time avoidance techniques!

OK, so we were then sent out in pairs to brainstorm creating the workshop—not the when, where and “how the hell will I market this?” but the passion, the stake upon which the workshop is based. The aha’s came thick and fast, with Karen and Kyle floating around giving advice and inspiration. Somehow, clarifying the stake connected us to our life purpose, shaking off the gremlin and making the business details quite manageable.

Having said that, with a brilliant piece of intuition, Karen came to me and answered questions that had sabotaged my workshops for a year: “You want three hours minimum, at least 10 people. Debrief. Break it down. Create the learning. Introduce a new learning piece. Point them on their way.” In 15 seconds, Karen sat on my saboteurs and sent me on my way.

We returned to the group to digest the learning, covering topics such as resistant groups. Every act is a cry for learning—“Hungry to learn but just don’t know how.” I need to be flexible enough to meet them and speak to the learner. We shared ideas on getting over nervousness, from tree-hugging (no, really!) to making a fool of yourself early on to get it out of the way! I was personally taken by a saboteur-busting phrase: “Who the hell am I to judge myself?!”

After a long lunch in the blazing hot sun, we returned for the afternoon session. This began with hot-seat coaching provided by Mandy, with Lulu as the coachee. This was breathtaking in its use of geography and took me out of my comfort zone as a coach, reminding us of the “fierce courage” that can be so important in coaching. Having been to ICF hot-seat coaching sessions, it once again demonstrated for me the brilliance of Co-Active coaches.

This was followed by Damien Churton and Joseph Seller, and “Your Natural Way to Get Clients” workshop. Having already taken this course, it was delightful to meet Damien and Joseph in person, and the refresher was great. The basis for their programme of telephone workshops is the fact that we all try to follow the instructions of “experts” for how to get clients, shutting off our own natural way. The lively session abounded with gardening metaphors and bubbled with exchange and laughter. Whether you were a flower or a bee, the transparent learning was quite a relief! When you notice and follow your natural way, then you sow seeds, nurture them and are on hand to help them bloom so that you can harvest!

All in all, an extremely enjoyable day, learning, laughing and meeting people for the first time that I feel I have known for years! Don’t miss the next one!

Love,

Jill McCullogh
