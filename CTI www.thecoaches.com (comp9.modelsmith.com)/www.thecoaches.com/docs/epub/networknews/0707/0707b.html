 Member Musings

Oh, the Places We’ve Been!

Shock. I’m scheduled to lead a course in Canada on August 28. My passport expires two weeks before that. Has it really been 10 years?

Panic. I heard it is taking forever to get renewals through. Will I have enough time?

Dread. Oh no! I have to get my picture taken. Yuck. Will I look 10 years older? And, I’m so hating my hair right now. There is nothing worse than being stuck with a bad photo for 10 years—especially on your passport. Especially if looking at it makes you feel like an old broad when you’re going abroad. But that’s just my saboteur. Or at least I decide that’s what it is. I go get my new photo.

Calm. I’ve filled out the application and I’m following my instructions. My picture didn’t turn out too bad, after all. I sigh as I write the check, adding in the extra $60 to expedite the process (whatever that means!).

 Ask Judith

Dear Judith,

What is your opinion about the Enneagram and do you know any important books about this system that you can recommend? Thanks.

—J.S.

Dear J.S.:
The Enneagram is just one of many personality typing systems that people have devised in order to better understand themselves and others. Don Riso, a noted Enneagram teacher and developer, has defined the Enneagram as “a geometric figure that delineates the nine basic personality types of human nature and their complex relationships.” The Enneagram is a useful tool for understanding oneself and others because it focuses not only upon types of behavior but it is also concerned with underlying attitudes, emotional reactions, conscious and unconscious motivations, as well as spiritual potentials.

The Gremlin is a concept developed by Richard Carson that embodies a group of thought processes and feelings that serve to maintain the status quo in our lives. Often operating as a structure that would seem to protect us, the Gremlin, in fact, keeps us from moving forward and getting what we truly want in life. Like our mind, the Gremlin will always be with us. It is neither good nor bad; it just is. The Gremlin loses its power over us when we can identify it for what it is, notice our options in the situation and then consciously choose what it is we really want at the time.

Topic: CTI® Skills Call—Holding the Client’s Agenda
July 18, 2007
12:30-1:30 p.m. Pacific, 8:30-9:30 p.m. GMT
Click here to RSVP

Holding the client's agenda is both a philosophical stance and a cornerstone in the Co-Active model. When coaches hold the client’s agenda, they let go of their own opinions, judgments and answers in support of facilitating the client's fulfillment, balance and process. Coaches follow the client’s lead without knowing the right answer, giving solutions or telling the client what to do. Holding the client's agenda requires coaches to put their whole attention on the client and his/her agenda, not on their own agenda for the client.
