Reflections on Authenticity
by Nick Fowler
Co-leader, Coaching in Organizations

It seems that everywhere I turn, I am hearing about the importance of “authenticity”—especially at work, where many of us “know” we can’t really be authentic. It wouldn’t be “appropriate.” It would be too risky. It could be career-limiting. So what is going on? This month’s Fast Company cover even cites the “Selling of Authenticity” by Nike, BMW and Starbucks.

What are we talking about here anyway? Webster defines authenticity as “true to one’s own personality, spirit or character,” and “not false or imitation.” On a recent niche call with about 50 people, we began by talking about authenticity, playing with different definitions and hearing different perspectives. Later on that same call, we heard a story about a challenge, a choice and the differences it had made for the teller. We shifted to a different level of listening and hearing. We felt and understood in a different way what authenticity really was.

Being Well-Used
by Carol Ross, CPCC, PCC

At the end of a 14-hour day, after working with a team of 24 people in a company, I looked over at my partner for this client gig, Deb Siverson. She spoke for both of us when she uttered the words, “I feel well-used.” 

We were at dinner with the team. The dinner capped a marathon week of email and phone conversations with the client, weekend afternoons on the phone with my partner analyzing team data and designing the day with the team, and working closely with a subset of the team beforehand to create allies and role models.

And now, we were celebrating breakthroughs in relationships that were unimaginable a week ago. Parties that had been polarized—disenfranchised team members and management—were willing to work together on long-standing problems. The “us vs. them” stance had softened enough so that eye contact could be made with the enemy and glasses raised for a toast. Cliques dissolved as teammates chose to move to other tables to mingle. Team members felt heard. It was truly remarkable.

 Network Support Wizardry

A Bit About Discussion Functionality

I’ve received a few emails asking about why, when entering the discussions, it doesn’t always take you to the most recent post in the listing. I thought I’d share this design feature with you in detail.

What the system does by default is drop you into the discussion somewhere that makes sense, depending on whether you are subscribed to it or not and whether or not you've read it all. If there are dozens of messages and you've read them all, does it make sense to drop you in at the beginning? What the system does in that case is drop you in toward the end. If it's a thread with hundreds of unread messages, in some circumstances it will drop you in at, say, 20 from the end, so you don't get overwhelmed reading all the old stuff.

You see, there are so many factors involved given the unique user experience of activity throughout the system.

Keep on talking!

 Ask Judith

Dear Judith:

My personal life is blowing up, and I can feel the impact this is having on my coaching. My presence is down; my curiosity is down; my ability to view myself as creative, resourceful and whole is down. I feel I'm not serving my clients well. Before my calls, I muster myself, but I feel the creep of the down-ness set in and hold it ALL down. Can you help shed light on managing this kind of situation?
—D.D.

Dear D.D.:
First and most importantly, what do you need in order to take care of yourself during this period of flux and upset? This is a traumatic time for you and, as is true of all people who are being traumatized, we need to take your needs into consideration first. So, do you need some time off to just be with what's going on? If you don't need some time off, do you need some hours each day to be with yourself or other supportive people? Do you need more time with your own coach? What support systems are available to you right now so that your needs are being adequately served? Never underestimate taking care of yourself. It can provide a helpful answer to so many of our problems.

Forwarding the action may occur through bringing the client back to the focus of his or her goal, or though reframing something in such a way that the client is free to take action.
