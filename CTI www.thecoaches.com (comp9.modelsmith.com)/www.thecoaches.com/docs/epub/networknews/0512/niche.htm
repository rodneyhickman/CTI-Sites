Rainbow Pride Niche

When we started the Rainbow Pride Niche for gay, lesbian, bisexual, transgender, tran-sexual, two-spirit, inter-sex and queer folks, it seemed like a good idea to get the rainbow up and flying on the Co-Active network. I experienced some personal trepidation at “coming out” to the whole coaching community. I wasn’t sure that I was the right person to lead the GLBT community—what do I know? Who am I to be a leader of this community? I don’t even have “gaydar”—that inner radar that tells you when someone else is gay. People have to come out to me over and over—and usually I need my partner to tell me that “I’m part of the family” or something similar doesn’t just mean they are friendly. That said, I have been learning something new. I am new at Co-Actively “leading” a community. I am new to being out on the Internet. I ask myself constantly, “What is important for the queer community in Co-Active space?” But today I am pondering what is important for the Co-Active community about queer space?

My partner and I have been watching back episodes of “Queer as Folk.” I am so curious about what it must be like for the straight actors to “be with” the steamy gay sex scenes and queer culture on the show. I wonder how their acceptance of their roles has changed them as human beings. “Queer as Folk” has been so educational for many of my straight and gay friends. It has brought queer life into gay homes and straight homes across North America. There is a huge demographic of straight women who watch “Queer as Folk.” I think of the impact of those straight actors playing gay roles. They have been more successful at social change in gay issues than many gay activists have been!

I wonder what impact we as queer coaches are having in the world. What kind of impact can we Rainbow Pride Co-Active Coaches have on the coaching community? I know that having a gay-friendly niche may allow someone queer to feel welcomed and able to participate in the broader community. We can use the Rainbow Coaches Niche to create a safe space for ourselves, a little community where it is OK to be part of the GLBT community. Our little space. But what if we can be bigger than this? What impact can we have on the greater community as a group of united queer coaches? What kind of impact would we like to have?

There are three areas that came to my mind today that queer culture might be able to contribute to coaching space, and I am sure there are many more. The first is in the area of authenticity. The gay culture can be so flamboyant…so beautiful…so big. There is so much permission in queer space. As coaches we help people be huge. What can we bring from our queer space to help people be huge? Think Drag Queen!

The second is in the area of courage. We model courage daily by living our lives openly. Courage to be out. Courage to ask for equal rights. Courage to marry and hold hands in public. Courage to march in a PRIDE parade with my three children. Courage to challenge laws that keep us small. Courage to live a full life.

The third area that I think gay culture has to offer coaching is in designing the alliance. In the queer world, negotiation and dialogue are more prominent in relationships than they are in straight culture—think of the hanky code. (For more info, Google “hanky code.”)  Straightforward communication is perspective-changing and life-changing. Is there anything we have to contribute from queer culture to the designed alliance?

What would excellence look like for us as Co-Active Queer Coaches? If we really called ourselves forth to be great coaches and great members of the GLBT community, what would we do? What impact would we have?

I would like to call you forth to look at the ways that you can have as much impact on the coaching community as a straight actor playing a gay man has had with “Queer as Folk.” If you are gay, are you making coaching available to your queer friends? Are you calling them forth fully? Are you living your own life in courage and authenticity? If you are straight, are you an advocate for gay rights? Have you learned how to be a gay advocate and ally? Are we, as Co-Active coaches, comfortable playing it safe in a small world, or are we ready to grow our impact? Are we ready to embrace all that we really are? Are we ready to reach for excellence and learn something new?

Melanie Parish is a business coach, trainer and keynote speaker who lives in Dundas, Ontario. She has a full coaching practice, a bed & breakfast and is married with three children. She is a co-leader of the Rainbow Pride Co-Active Coaches Niche.
