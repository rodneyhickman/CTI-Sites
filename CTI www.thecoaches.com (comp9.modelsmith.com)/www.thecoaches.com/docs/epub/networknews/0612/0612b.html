Rainbow Pride Co-Active Coaches
by Melanie Parish

As a co-leader of the Rainbow Pride niche of the Co-Active Network, I have seen how the GLBT community has much to contribute to the world of coaching. (GLBT, for you straight people out there, is gay, lesbian, bisexual and transgender.) The topics we discuss are applicable for everyone in the coaching profession. The gifts the GLBT community has are vast, in part, because of the challenges we face in our everyday lives.

There are several big contributions the GLBT community has to make to the world of coaching:

  1. Living a full life in the presence of detractors or homophobia or saboteurs.
  2. Support for those who are “coming out” and stepping into authenticity.
  3. Transition from where we are to where we want to be.
  4. Understanding of the myriad perspectives that make up human sexuality and humanness.

CTI Metro DC Geographic Community
by Michele Roden-Spruill, CPCC, PCC

Co-Activity is a feeling. It’s an energy that people want to be around. People who have experienced Co-Active coaching, a CTI course, the CTI booth or events at the ICF Conference get it. Clients who experience Co-Actively trained coaches and leaders know it is different. And the Co-Active Network is different because of it.

What would happen to the world if CTI coaches met as a community each month?

The answer, it turns out, is a powerful one…powerful enough to change a person’s life, move a community forward and connect the world.

But when Charlie Kilczewski, CPCC, first asked that question in 2001, we weren’t quite sure what to expect. But by asking the question, he inspired 20 Co-Active coaches in the metropolitan Washington, D.C., area to meet for the first time, curious as to what would unfold. The result was powerful stuff: a consistent time and space for people to meet, the opportunity to try a variety of potluck foods and, most importantly, the ability to build a community in every sense of the word.

Molting Season
by Gilly Weinstein

I’ve been spending a lot of time researching crabs lately. The larger kind, those tough, truly bizarre creatures that don’t seem to need anyone or anything. Garbage men of the sea, symbols for cancer, meanness incarnate, finger-lickin’ feast… Crabs conjure up the most diverse cultural and emotional associations. Including, as it turns out for me, an apt reminder of what growing stronger—or just plain growing—means.

What started as a high-intensive-effort to help my daughter with a school project blossomed into a full-blown fascination. Thanks to the whims of a moody science teacher (“This sucks! All the cool invertebrates were taken, now I’m stuck with the stupid crab!”), I now know more about these critters than anyone needs to. Like, get this: crabs continue to grow incessantly throughout their entire life. This steady expansion is what causes them to molt, i.e. crack their shell, extract themselves from it and move on—up to 20 times in a modest three-year life span. They do this in different ways. Mostly, they flush their innards so thoroughly with water that they literally pop out of themselves. Within 30 minutes, less in some cases, the crab is alleviated of his or her old shell and experiences the true condition of an invertebrate—no skeleton, all flesh, pretty raw.

 Network Support Wizardry

Hello Fellow Technical Friends,

You cannot rewrite history…

You know when you post a discussion and it says, “You cannot rewrite history, but you will have 30 minutes to make any changes or fixes after you post a message. Just click on the Edit button which follows your message after you post it.”

Within the 30 minutes, deleting it prevents it from going out in the digest, or from viewing it on the website. But for those of you who have your email set to “real time” notification, you will get them all—even if they end up not existing on the site or have been edited a few times.

So, the learning here is not to post to the site until you have the proper look, feel and wording set that you desire.

Let’s get grounded on this one aspect and I’ll see you next month!

Andrew Gabel
networksupport@thecoaches.com

 Ask Judith

Dear Judith:

I have a client who is in a very demanding doctoral program and he is rapidly becoming less and less tolerant of his mistakes and imperfections. Although he has been a perfectionist in the past, I want to work with him to become more aware of this tendency before it overwhelms him and sabotages his graduate school experience. Do you have any antidotes for “being perfect?”
—JZ

Dear JZ,
Fortunately, “being perfect” will never need an antidote because perfection is an ideal state and thus to my mind can never be fully reached. Perfection can, however, serve as a calling forth of our best selves. In that way, it can be honored both as an ideal and as a tool.
