Co-Active Coaching for Health/Vitality/Wellness

I often get puzzled looks when I tell people I am a professional life coach who works in the area of weight loss. “What does life coaching have to do with a diet?” I’m asked.

And I love it when they ask that question! After all, they are being curious and I’ve got lots to tell them about the topic. I love the opportunity to educate people about a tool that goes way beyond the limited scope of a diet.

Here’s what I know: If we adopt a diet or an exercise plan, it works temporarily, while we are following it. Once adherence (as the fitness community is fond of calling it) waivers, the weight comes back, as if it’s been on a little hike and is coming home to rest. If we change deeper attitudes about food (and ourselves and even life!), permanent change occurs.

The Atlantic Shrinks: Co-Activity Spreads in Europe

The Atlantic is shrinking fast and may soon evaporate. It feels as if, soon, we’ll be able to just step over it. We old Europeans, and I’m talking about ancient cultures rather than ancient coaches, have sometimes felt a little isolated. After all, coaching was “invented” in America and we’re a decade behind the times. As recently as a couple of years ago, there were doubts that we might ever be able to stand on our own two feet and contribute fully to the great international community of Co-Activity. Not any more. Things are happening quickly. Our sapling continues to shoot upwards and spread its roots.

The number of countries holding Co-Active coaching courses grows almost by the month. The leadership programme is safely transplanted across the pond. And we’re holding our sixth European Co-Active Coaches community day in May—to which you’re all invited.

 Member Musings

How do you prepare for a client?

Do a few Sun Salutations? Cordon yourself off from the kids and the dog? Make a nice cuppa tea? Sit in a special “coaching” chair? Or merely note the time, run to the phone and wing it?

As a new coach, I have gone the gamut on my rituals for coaching. Sometimes, I sit at my desk until two minutes before the client is to call, madly checking e-mails and feverishly surfing my yahoo. Or I’ve taken the thoughtful approach: light a nice, smelly candle, spend 15 minutes reviewing the client’s notes and homework assignments, and make a rough plan of the things I want to remember to cover (a.k.a. remind of late payment!). I’ve coached from my office, with the window overlooking tall trees, or from the leisure of the living room couch, or even sitting cross-legged on my bed, propped up with many pillows. I’ve been in my PJs, or full-on “hard clothes” and shoes. I’ve taken calls back-to-back or splat in the middle of a day of nothing going on.

 Ask Judith

Dear Judith:

I am a new coach and I am feeling a bit overwhelmed by all of my clients. I think about them a lot during the week. I can’t stop wondering about them and what is happening for them during the time between our coaching sessions. Is this typical? More importantly, how does one stop thinking about one’s clients all the time?
—S.T.

Dear S.T.:
This seems to be a typical phase that many new coaches go through when they start their coaching practices. On one hand, it is a good sign that you care about your clients and are committed to doing a good job. On the other hand, these concerned thoughts may be sourced by your saboteurs and, thus, need to be managed.
