Safari Miracles

by Linda Lea, PhD, CPCC

“I am somewhere in the future and I am much better than I am right now. Say YES to the right and say NO to the wrong.”
— Leah Mwangi, Grassroots African Women’s Conference, Bondo, Kenya, December 2006

The word safari in Kiswahili means to go on a journey or expedition. My recent safari to East Africa in December 2006, was a series of miracles along the way. Never did I dream that my coaching and leadership education I completed five years ago, would lead me to the first rural Grassroots African Women’s Conference in Bondo, Kenya. My journey to East Africa was to give back to young women some of the gifts that I had been given.

The world is waking up to the importance of educating girls, and my work in Tanzania and Kenya with impoverished rural girls is on the cutting edge of this awakening. The programs that I have created for Maasai girls in East Africa have grown much bigger than my original goal to start a scholarship program. Five years ago, I had a vision to create a program for secondary school girls in Tanzania. The impetus came from my Leadership training and the opportunity from Global Partners for Development, a nonprofit working in East Africa. Out of this profound experience, an idea was born: take the training that I had been given and pass it on to girls who would have no opportunity to become leaders in their own lives. I started with a small pilot in 2002 of 43 girls and today I have enrolled 320 young women into the program. In 2004, we expanded into Kenya, and in 2007, we are headed to Uganda.

The idea for the first Grassroots African Women’s Conference was born out of the desire to give rural women a voice in designing their future. When I first heard about it, the thought I had was “Of course!” The conference presented a perfect opportunity to introduce leadership training for some of my scholarship girls. It was a vision that I had held for a very long time.

The theme for the conference was empowering women in issues that affected them on a daily basis: HIV/AIDS, human trafficking, micro loans, illiteracy and economic development. Since the beginning of the scholarship program, it was my vision to empower the African community to make decisions on the girls’ behalf. It was, as I could see, the only way for the program to become self-sustaining. Thus, I let Africa decide who would be chosen to go to the conference based on their criteria. When the time came and I actually met the girls in Bondo, the excitement was electric. The 14 girls that attended had never been out of their villages and now they were at a conference with more than 500 rural African women, learning about important issues that would impact their lives.

During the next four days, I worked with a team of women who were also at the conference and did three workshops with the scholarship girls. The first one was on communication. I modeled how a simple introduction could be powerful. This exercise gave me an opportunity to really listen from another perspective, taking into account the cultural differences. I sat back and let them evaluate each other. By the end of the first workshop, they had formed a community, working together to practice their newly learned skills.

On the second day, the girls came in excited about what they were learning at the conference. They worked in pairs to put on paper concepts, insights and ideas, and it was inspiring to watch them write down what was important to them. For the first time, they had the freedom to design and create without being judged.

The third workshop was a celebration, as one of the girls had a birthday. Thus, we decided to give her a party as a reward for the hard work that had been done there. It was the last day that we would be working with them and we wanted it to be special. They squealed with joy as they ate cake and talked about being together for the last time. We also worked that day on using what they had learned to take back to their community. In small groups, the girls came up with projects that they would initiate based on the themes from the conference.

For the six from Tanzania, it was a flight home, to attend the 3rd Education Forum in Arusha. Their role was to present to the community and their respective schools what they had learned. Thus, it was at the Forum that I witnessed the birth of self-confidence and empowerment that would hopefully be a part of their daily lives. They did not disappoint me. More than 200 people attended, including two members of Parliament. The girls stood up in front of the audience, hands trembling and talked about having a future with the right to be heard. It was remarkable, and I will never forget the passion and the power of their words. This experience was the culmination of a seed that had been planted five years ago in my Leadership Training. Today, it is a reality.

If I hadn’t taken the initiative to “leap” into a vast new place to play, I would not have had the honor to meet and work with these incredible young women. It is my belief that coaches come to this work for different reasons, and often what they discover is wrapped in a dazzling package.

Linda Lea, Ph.D, CPCC is a former professor, an international education consultant, a Certified Professional Co-Active Coach, lecturer and author. She has traveled extensively for her literacy work in East Africa, and is currently an Education Niche organizer for CTI.
