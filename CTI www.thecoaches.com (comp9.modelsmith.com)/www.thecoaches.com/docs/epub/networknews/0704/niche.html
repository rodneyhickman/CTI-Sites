Of Birds, Bees…and Coaches

It’s everywhere. In advertising, on the message boards of the Co-Active Network, at the movies, on magazine racks.

Yup, even at church. Two weeks ago, I received an oversized postcard from the local evangelical church announcing in large, bold font above the smiling, boyish face of the local preacher, who, in my opinion anyway, looks a bit young to be an authority on such things: “God Wants You to Have Great Sex!”

In the past couple of months, there have been a number of posts on the Co-Active boards dealing with sex, sexuality and sexual energies.

The responses to those posts have represented voices of warning, gratitude, abandon, danger, creativity, fear, freedom and more. In other words, they have been all over the map.

What is it about something as common as sex, in a community peopled by champions of self-expression and bearers of the flame of the full range of human experience that leads to such highly charged reactions?

Quite frankly, I don’t know that I have answers to that question.

What I’m fairly certain of is that sex, sexuality and the energies that surround, infuse and inform all things sexual are—for those interested and willing to part with their comfort levels, let go of their assumptions and venture beyond the pale of the prurient and puritanical (and trust the Co-Active Model)—rich worlds worthy of exploration on both personal and, as coaches, professional levels.

It would be naïve to say that, just because human beings procreate sexually and are all the products of sexual union, there isn’t anything to discuss outside mechanics and biological fact. Were that the case, I could end the article here and go watch TV.

Of course, human sexuality is far more complex than that.

There is no shortage of confusion when it comes to sexual issues. In the wake of the women’s and men’s movements—to say nothing of the Sexual Revolution, the age of HIV/AIDS and an increasingly embraced (on one level) yet held at arm’s length relationship with gay and lesbian culture on the part of the main-stream (“Queer Eye” and “Ellen” are entertaining, but is it OK for “those” people to marry or live next door?)—we see and hear more about sex and sexuality than ever before. Still, as a society, we don’t do a very good job of holding it all or engaging in open, curious conversation on the subjects of sex or sexuality.

And we are all male or female sexual beings.

While that may not be front-page news, it is very easy to overlook the fact that, as humans, we experience life in one of two primary ways: either via a male body or a female body. While it may be politically incorrect to reduce experience to “male” and “female,” the simple fact remains. As men and women, our experiences of the world are not homogeneous.

Water is wet, rocks are hard, and men and women see and feel the world differently.

I would assert that, as coaches, we do our clients—male and female—a huge disservice by assuming that differences as elemental as gender can be glossed over. Of course, our clients are human beings, and their “human” experience is informed, sometimes in subtle ways, sometimes at high-volume, by whether they look at the world through a male or female lens!

Our clients have names, accomplishments, goals, foibles, dreams…and a specific gender—all of which are worthy of honor and attention.

Does that mean that as a coach, I can access some sort of formula that frees me from having to be curious or can serve as a form of pre-determined template for engagement, based on whether a client is a man or a woman?

Not in the least. If anything, it means there is even more to be curious about—at an “essence” level that most people don’t explore. Gender is such an elemental component of who we are. It’s something so obvious that we are often unconscious as to how much it impacts and defines our experience.

Another point I would like to make is that sex and sexuality are not pathologies.

Many people have a difficult time being in the presence of emotion, and so it is with topics of a sexual nature. Sex is one of those areas in which an uninformed, largely prudish society has, paradoxically, made it all but unacceptable not to know. Assuming that people instinctually know about sex is akin to assuming that the waiter knows what you’ll be ordering for dinner before you’ve walked in the door.

Preferences and desires are as varied as the individuals who have them, yet permission to try new things or even talk about experimentation can feel like dangerous terrain. Combine that reticence with shame or guilt and social expectations, and watch the sexual saboteurs (gremlins) arrive by the bus-load.

Naturally creative, resourceful and whole people with a desire to discuss and explore their sexual attractions, fantasies or nature are not broken. They needn’t be ushered off to therapy simply because they wish to explore their sexuality. (Sexual violence and/or abuse, physical or otherwise—as either victim or perpetrator—is a different matter entirely.)

Not all coaches are comfortable with sexual issues, and it isn’t important that all coaches develop the skills or curiosity to work with clients in this arena. If you have no interest in working with clients who bring sexual subjects to the table, make that clear to your clients when and if the subject comes up, and offer to refer them on.

Having said the above, if your own personal beliefs or history make it impossible for you to self-manage, to see them as naturally creative, resourceful and whole, to deal with a client’s sexuality without judging them or introducing your own agenda into the mix, notice that. If there is something in you that needs to be healed, by all means find the appropriate professional and do your work.

Last, and certainly not least, human beings have—thank you Carl Jung!—shadows, and they often hide the best of themselves in dark places.

In the years the Masculine/Feminine/Third Way group has been working together, I continue to be amazed and moved by the transformations that occur when men and women stop running from the parts of themselves they have “sequestered off-stage in a sound-proof booth,” as the line from the old TV show used to go.

Sexual shadows are often viewed as frighteningly dark and dangerous. They typically present facades of overwhelming, uncontrollable force and/or powerless vulnerability. They can be hidden beneath a client’s fear that, should they crack the door just a bit, it will be thrown open and they will be swallowed.

To be sure, in a coaching situation, facing those shadows takes a high level of commitment and intentionality from both coach and client, and sure-footed and focused levels of listening, self-management and curiosity on the part of the coach. The agenda for that level of exploration, of course, comes from the client. I have yet to see a client walk through the dark and “eat” their shadow—sexual or otherwise—who has not come out the other side intact and, in fact, stronger, more present, alive and fulfilled as a result.

There is, of course, much more to be said about sex, sexuality, sexual energy and coaching than we have room for here.

The bottom line is that sex is and will remain a normal, healthy component of human life and relationships so long as we continue to walk the Earth. As with every other aspect of the human experience—male or female—questions will arise, concerns will surface and a multitude of perspectives will be generated.

If I could offer just one piece of advice in the arena of working with sexuality, it is this: lean into the Co-Active model for, as with human sexuality, there is much to learn there. Coach on!
