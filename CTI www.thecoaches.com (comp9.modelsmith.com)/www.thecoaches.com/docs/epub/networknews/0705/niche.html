Reflections on Authenticity

by Nick Fowler, Mollee Johnston and Linda Kennedy co-leaders, Coaching in Organizations

It seems that everywhere I turn, I am hearing about the importance of “authenticity”—especially at work, where many of us “know” we can’t really be authentic. It wouldn’t be “appropriate.” It would be too risky. It could be career-limiting. So what is going on? This month’s Fast Company cover even cites the “Selling of Authenticity” by Nike, BMW and Starbucks.

What are we talking about here anyway? Webster defines authenticity as “true to one’s own personality, spirit or character,” and “not false or imitation.” On a recent niche call with about 50 people, we began by talking about authenticity, playing with different definitions and hearing different perspectives. Later on that same call, we heard a story about a challenge, a choice and the differences it had made for the teller. We shifted to a different level of listening and hearing. We felt and understood in a different way what authenticity really was.

Why is authenticity important in organizations? From a very pragmatic perspective, organizations are always seeking ways to be more efficient and effective, to improve quality and to provide a more satisfying experience for their employees, customers and suppliers. We have tried, and we learn in business schools, many techniques and tools for addressing these needs, including such things as LEAN, Six-Sigma, recognition programs and high performance work teams. All of these fall short of their potential because we choose within the cultures we are co-creating to hold back a lot of who we are—to stay in our heads and avoid what our hearts have to offer. As we bring even 10% of who we really are to engage in our work, we will see dramatic results.

How do we, as coaches and coach-like leaders invite or encourage authenticity in organizations? What is the impact of our modeling with every client or team? How do we design our alliance in each of our relationships in the organization—and redesign it as it evolves?

How are we authentic in our own lives? It seems to me that we choose to be authentic. Sometimes that choice is in the moment, while interacting with someone. We practice noticing when we are straying from authenticity—covering up or faking something—and choose again. We also create an intention to be authentic and make choices in advance that support that intention, such as getting enough sleep and protecting ourselves from stress or other situations that would impair our ability to be authentic.

I believe it helps to spend time with authentic people who remind us of how authenticity feels. People such as: Henry Kimsey-House (listen to Henry talk about curiosity), Pema Chodren (listen to her talking of awakening compassion) or Rachel Naomi Remen (read her My Grandfather’s Blessings). And notice who you are when you are being authentic. A few weeks ago, I participated in a Conversation Café (see www.conversationcafe.org) for 90 minutes with some strangers in our town. At the end, someone asked, “What is this relationship we have created? It feels so much deeper than friendship, almost like love.” I believe that level of intimacy was possible because we were authentic. What is possible as we create and maintain relationships at this level in all aspects of our lives, even in our organizations?

How are you being authentic today?

How are you not, and what can you learn from that?

What comes up for you as you reflect on authenticity?

Who else could you learn from to enhance your authenticity?

What symbol or word helps you remember to be authentic?

What stories do you have to share?
