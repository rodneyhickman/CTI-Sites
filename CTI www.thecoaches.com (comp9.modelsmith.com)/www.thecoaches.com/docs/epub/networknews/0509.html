The New Hero

When we think of heroes that we have known throughout time, we reflect on people who have left an enormous impact on the world through some kind of personal sacrifice. Often, what comes to mind are men and women who gave their time and energy to make a difference that has been recorded in time. Presidents, saints, visionaries are all part of the history and mythology that surrounds heroes. And maybe, just maybe, there are people right here in our own lives that need to be recognized as our personal heroes.

What is a hero?
The American Heritage Dictionary defines a hero as: “Any man noted for feats of courage or nobility of purpose, especially one who has risked or sacrificed his life.” Today, the word hero no longer has this exact dictionary definition. I believe we have a new perception of the word “hero,” redefining our image of who actually qualifies. The word “new” intimates that something is different. Somehow, the old way of thinking about what a hero is no longer exists. The new heroes may be humanitarians, serving others, inspiring people in different ways by how they live their lives. It may be a person who is committed to changing something in his or her community by personal example. Passion is there, as well and a willingness to be selfless in the desire to bring about change.

Greater Seattle Co-Active Coaches Community

“Another coaching group in the Seattle area?” a colleague in another field recently asked me. “Why do you need another group?” She was confused. She was not a coach. She had never attended a CTI course. But I know that’s a question on several other coach’s minds in the Greater Seattle area. There is already a strong ICF chapter called the Puget Sound Coaches Association (PSCA), which does a wonderful job of bringing all the local coaches together and offers excellent networking opportunities, guest speakers and panel discussions. The Seattle area is also home to the Academy for Coach Training in Bellevue, WA, which is owned and operated by a CTI graduate and Master Certified Coach, Fran Fisher. So, why a CTI geographic community in the Seattle area?

To answer such a simple question, think back to your experiences at CTI. What were some of your favorite courses? The Co-Active Coaching Course (Fundamentals)? Fulfillment? Balance? Process? Synergy? Relationship Coaching? Certification? Leadership? What connections did you make in those classes with other students and the leaders? Do you still live from the place of your Future Self? How BIG is your coaching or how BIG are you showing up in your life?

“You’re Too…!”
(Intense, Emotional, Hard-Driving, Idealistic…)

Ouch! This feedback strikes at a gut level. It may be surprising that what you consider normal is perceived by others as extreme. This disconnect occurs when your unique qualities manifest with more intensity than that of the average person. The irony is that you feel criticized for something fundamental to your success! People with this “too” intensity hear things like:

  • You’re too much of a perfectionist; you can’t expect us to keep up.
  • You’re too goal-oriented; you only care about results, not people.
  • You’re too busy; you never have time for friends or family.

Ignoring these perceptions can restrict your career options, as well as personal relationships. The challenge is pausing long enough to recognize your current impact and identify your desired one.

Take Chris* for example, who earned a critical role based on superb attention to detail, great intelligence and unparalleled dedication. This intensity, however, yielded an unanticipated result. Chris’ “efficient” directness was perceived as condescending, and colleagues worried about getting on Chris’ “bad side.” People said Chris was too task-oriented and too fast-paced. There was also significant personal impact. As the workday grew longer and longer, there was less attention to family, individual rejuvenation and personal health, which was suffering.

Dear Judith:

My client always finds a way to blame somebody else for his misfortunes. Granted, people have not always been kind to him, but he never sees that he contributes to what is happening to him. Do you have any suggestions for working with this kind of client?

Z.N.

Dear Z.N.:

I believe that most of us would like to shift perceived blame from ourselves to other people. After all, who wants to feel bad about the actions that we may or may not have taken? However, blame is not particularly useful when creating learning, especially in painful situations. The most important thing that you can do in working with a client around this issue is to establish that there is more than one point of view about the situation in which he finds himself. Interestingly, there are clients who have the same problem in reverse. They take the blame for everything rather than realistically assessing a situation to determine what is going on.
