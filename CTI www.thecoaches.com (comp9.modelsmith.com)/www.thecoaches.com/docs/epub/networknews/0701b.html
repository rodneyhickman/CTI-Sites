Coaching Impacts Recovery Community
by Alida Schuyler, Haskell Hestand, Theresa Swift

Addiction costs Americans more than $100 billion a year in lost productivity, social disorder and excessive use of healthcare services. This affects everyone: health insurers and public health agencies see disproportionate use of emergency rooms, general medical and psychiatric services; employers pay for substance abuse treatment and need their employees to return to full functioning; and the criminal justice system wants less crime, incarceration and parole violations.

At home, families want to live without the worries, embarrassment, violence and financial concerns that go hand-in-hand with substance abuse. And addicts themselves have needs and wants. They want relief from discomfort caused by the direct effects of substance abuse, as well as improved personal and social functioning, resulting in fewer problems. Everyone wants the costs of addiction to come down, especially the cost of repeated treatment. Unfortunately, the majority of addicts relapse soon after treatment.

What Can You Do with Silence?
by Rikki Fowler, CPCC, PCC

Discover the deliciousness of silence. Allow silence after a client speaks and listen to what emerges into the silence. During a telephone call, let the silence increase and be amazed at the wisdom that surfaces. In a workshop, provide silence after those provocative questions. What do you do with silence?

Did you take some time to think about the question? Or did you say, I’m too busy?

What is silence? It’s the absence of sound; a time when we hear something, like the breeze or a coyote howl, that makes us aware of the silence around us; a quiet mind that allows you to be aware of the external silence; and the internal stillness from which another voice, not your own, speaks.

 Network Support Wizardry

Hello Friends,

I’ve been getting some inquires for support lately from members who are noticing that the email seems to be quieter than usual. They’re wondering if email traffic has changed significantly on the site.

Well, I can say that it hasn’t changed any more than the natural trends of the impact of holidays. Check in with your email provider to ensure that any email address with “@coactivenetwork.com” is listed in your “white pages.” The email system on the Co-Active Network has not changed and 99.9% of the time is not responsible for you not getting email notifications from it. There are myriad applications, spam filters, server systems, etc., that often modify their settings ongoingly. There are also members who have email forwarding features/rules and multiple accounts on the site that have an impact on email notifications.

For example, networksupport@thecoaches.com has not been receiving email notifications from any niche or geographic community for about a month. After emails to my tech support, it was once again proven to be on my user-side of things. Our IT guy had upgraded my system with an application that unintentionally thwarted the delivery of emails from the Co-Active Network in service of clearing other spam from being received. Lots of learning!

Of course, I’ll do my best and am happy to work with you with any site-related issues. And there are times when the “mystery” of technology baffles me, too! Thank you for your emails of acknowledgement, support and love. We’re having a lot of fun with this global community. Aren’t we?

Happy, happy,

Andrew Gabel
networksupport@thecoaches.com

 Ask Judith

Dear Judith:

It’s the beginning of the New Year and most of my clients are busy making resolutions that I am sure will be forgotten at some point before the end of 2007. How can I support my clients in their resolve to change? —N. S.

Dear N. S.,
I have two immediate answers to your question, both of which come from different perspectives. My first answer is one that probably will not sound like a coach-like response. I’m not sure that change in the context that you have mentioned it merits such a good reputation. When I think about how long it has taken me to make lasting changes in my life, it usually has been a long and drawn out process with many stops and starts. In addition, the motivating factors for the change were usually external and not particularly organic. I know this experience is not personal to me. My clients have also had similar experiences of change.
