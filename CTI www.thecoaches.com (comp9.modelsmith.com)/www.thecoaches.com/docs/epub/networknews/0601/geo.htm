Louisville (Lū • ă • vûl) or Looavul, Luhvul, Loueville, Looaville and Looeyville

“So, you’re a coach—what sport? Until recently, that was the usual response to questioning about professional coaching in Louisville. Louisville, you see, is a hotbed of college basketball—U of L, UK and IU. If you happen to be a basketball virgin and don’t recognize these legendary teams or their coaches (Rick Pitino, Tubby Smith, Bobby Knight and Denny Crum), then perhaps you’ll recognize other sports names celebrated by our great city: Muhammad Ali, the Louisville Slugger, the Valhalla Golf Course, Louisville Extreme (skateboard) Park, and the ever popular, Kentucky Derby. Keeneland is “just down the road” in Lexington, and within a two-hour drive from Louisville are the Cincinnati Reds, the Cincinnati Bengals, the Indianapolis Colts, the Indiana Pacers and the Indianapolis Motor Speedway.

Today, the coaching profession in Louisville is finding recognition outside the field of sports. While variations of the nine graduates of CTI have been gathering at Heine Bros. Coffee shops for the last six years, a dedicated trio has set out to create a “buzz” in 2006 about our Coaching Alliance and the profession of coaching in Louisville. By joining the CTI network as the newest geographic community, we take our first step in becoming the premier professional coaching hub of the southeast. As founding members of the Kentuckiana Coach Alliance, we have been catalysts in the reformation of our local chapter of the ICF. (Which, by the way, has relocated its headquarters to Lexington, KY, a mere hour’s drive to the east.) Since I’ve been doing a bit of name-dropping, guess what CTI legend is from our neck of the woods? David Darst and Chuck Roppel are both from Louisville and great guesses, but not the one I have in mind. Read on to find the answer.

What better place than Louisville to emerge as the coaching hub of the southeast? It’s the largest city in Kentucky and the sixteenth largest city in the United States. Located on the banks of the Ohio River, Louisville borders southern Indiana, thus the ICF geographic descriptive, Kentuckiana. Unlike Chicago, St. Louis and Nashville, located less than 300 miles from here, Louisville has a small town charm and a lower cost of living, with many of the amenities of the larger cities.

Sometimes called the “Gateway to the South,” Louisville’s way of life represents a mixture of the South, the Midwest and the Southeast. Our neighborhoods encompass rich traditions from the Russian, French, German, Bosnian, Irish, African, Mexican and Vietnamese cultures. We are part of the Olmstead Park System, hosting three of Frederick Olmstead’s flagship parks.

Other little known facts about Louisville borrowed from the Louisville Metro website include:

  • The City of Louisville was founded in 1778 by George Rogers Clark and is named after King Louis XVI of France.
  • Louisville's First Town Charter was signed in 1780 by Thomas Jefferson, Governor of Virginia.
  • The Louisville arts community has gained international acclaim and is one of only nine U.S. cities with a professional opera, ballet, theatre, orchestra and children's theatre.
  • The first hand transplant in the United States was performed in Louisville.
  • The University of Louisville, founded in 1828, is the oldest city-supported college in the United States.
  • Chewing gum was invented by Louisvillian John Colgan. In 1873, Colgan created, but did not patent, his "Taffy Julie."
  • The Hot Brown originated at The Brown Hotel in downtown Louisville in the 1900s. The open-faced turkey and bacon sandwich is topped with a rich cheese sauce.
  • Built in 1914, the Belle of Louisville is the oldest operating Mississippi-style sternwheeler steamboat in existence today.
  • The cheeseburger originated at Kaelin's Restaurant on Newburg Road. In 1934, Margaret Kaelin's husband asked her to put a slice of American cheese on his hamburger and the legend was born.
  • "Happy Birthday to You" was written by sisters Mildred and Patty Hill in their Louisville kindergarten class. Originally called "Good Morning to You," it was later changed to this tradition birthday anthem.
  • Jennie Carter Benedict, a Louisville restaurateur, created Benedictine, a spread made of cucumbers and cream cheese.
  • Louisville's Main Street has the second largest collection of cast-iron storefronts facades; only New York City has more.

Major businesses headquartered in Louisville include UPS, KFC, Humana, Brown-Foreman and Kindred Healthcare. Ford Motor Company has two assembly plants here, and GE has an appliance division. In addition, Louisville is the home of the Presbyterian Church USA, the Southern Baptist Theological Seminary and the Louisville Presbyterian Theological Seminary.
