So this is February, and the big holiday that most of us are preparing for is Valentine’s Day.

Now, I’m not going to spend a lot of time digging into the historical facts of how this tradition began, nor focus on the theories of how it’s really a marketing scam perpetuated by greeting card, chocolate and flower companies (the deadly romantic three).

No, it seems quite uncoach-like to bring up all that when there’s a bountiful topic that really needs some examination and attention. Relationships.

It does seem quite coach-like to explore how to further enrich and embrace the relationships that we do have.

Obviously, Valentine’s Day is that yearly reminder for many of us to celebrate that big intimate relationship of ours in the form of presents and special dinners out to exquisite French restaurants. For those who don’t have one to celebrate, there seems to be this “less than” attribute, as though something is wrong if you don’t have someone to spend hundreds of dollars on this high holy day of love. How ridiculous. Valentines Day is kind of like the “Sneeches”—a constant comparison between those who have and have not.  And who is better?

Really, Valentines Day is not about all the jewelry and vacations and sweets, like we see on TV. Valentine’s Day is about taking time out to appreciate that intimate relationship, any and all of them, that is so important to us. Take away the cards, the candy, the dinners out, the lingerie, the diamonds and all those tokens of expression, and what is left? The relationship.

So I propose that we change February to National Relationship Month. Heck, we’re already devoting one entire day to an intimate relationship, why not take advantage and celebrate all the many other wonderful and valued relationships that ebb and flow in our lives with the other 27 days?

So I’ve come up with a couple of challenges for you all to take on this month.

Challenge #1 is about acknowledging and valuing your current relationships.

Take out a sheet of paper and write down all the relationships you can think of: intimate, family, friends, neighbors, co-workers, community, all the people that you interact and connect with. Now take out your calendar/datebook/Blackberry and put a different relationship in each day of February. (The 14th has already been done for most of you.)  Now, when that day comes around, you get to focus your attention on that relationship. What works about it? What doesn’t work? What can you count on them for? What can they count on you for? Pick three words that describe the essence of your relationship with that person. And perhaps take some time that day to make contact with him or her. You don’t have to buy them all a card or gift or even do anything except give yourself a little time to appreciate and love that relationship.

Think about it. At then end of the month, you will have given yourself the gift of connecting with 28 important people in your life.
