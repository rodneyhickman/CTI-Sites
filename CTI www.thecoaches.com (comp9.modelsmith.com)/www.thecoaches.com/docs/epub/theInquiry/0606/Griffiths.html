You’ve Been Asked to Give a Workshop—Now What?

Mary had accepted an invitation to present a workshop. But when she sat down to prepare the content, she felt like her thoughts were about as inspiring as the blank computer screen staring back at her.

“I’m sure these people have attended workshops on this topic before. How can I possibly make it new and interesting for them?” she moaned. “Come to think of it, why did I even accept this project in the first place?”

Ever been there? Me too—and that’s how I learned to use a valuable tool that I call “Operation Brain Dump.”

Now, before you say, “Yes, but first there has to be something in my brain to dump,” let me assure you that you have a lot to offer and people are waiting to hear it.

As coaches, we are trained to ask great questions. And that’s exactly where to start when creating a winner workshop. Before I give you the step-by-step process to help you effectively use “Operation Brain Dump,” here are three tips with useful questions to ask prior to writing your workshop content.

  1. Be curious. If someone invited you to deliver your workshop to their group, don’t try to prepare it in the fog by assuming you know their organization. Find out as much as you can about them. Go to their website. Talk to people who are members. Read their promotional materials.
  2. Be Co-Active. The Co-Active coaching model that we use with our clients is also applicable to preparing and delivering a workshop. Start by designing an alliance with the organizers who asked you to lead the workshop. Ask them questions. Find out the purpose of the overall event that your workshop is part of and what their goal is for your workshop.
  3. Be focused. Don’t try to prepare your material for the masses. When you attempt to reach everyone, you end up reaching no one. Again, ask questions of the organizers. What can they tell you about the people who will be attending the workshop? Who are they—really? What kind of experience would the planners like the attendees to have? What would they like them to take away from the workshop?

When you’ve taken these tips to heart and done your homework, you’ll be in a better position to connect your message to the audience’s needs. You’ll know what illustrations and metaphors they’ll relate to. You’ll know what activities and visuals will appeal to them. And you’ll be clearer on what your focus needs to be.

You’re also likely to feel like you have more information and ideas than you can handle. That’s where “Operation Brain Dump” comes in.

Step 1: Gather the right tools: 2” x 2” post-it notes, felt pen, blank wall in your home or office.

Step 2: Write one thought (maximum four words) on a post-it note and stick it on the wall. Don’t reason the thought away; just “dump” it. If a thought comes up that doesn’t seem to relate to your workshop, put it on the wall anyway. You never know how it might fit in later.

Step 3: Repeat this process until all your thoughts are out (one thought per post-it note). Don’t try to organize the post-its; just get them on the wall. If you have a thought that’s similar to one you’ve already posted, write it down anyway and put it on the wall. Different wording can stimulate other perspectives and lead you down new paths of thinking.

Step 4: By the time you’ve downloaded everything you can think of, you will have a menagerie of sticky notes all over your wall. Now step back and look for the common threads.

Step 5: Organize the common thoughts by moving post-it notes into one-theme clusters or groups. If another idea comes up while you’re doing this, jot it down and put it on the wall. If there are items that don’t seem to relate to anything about your workshop, put them in a group by themselves. They may end up being useful as illustrations or just miscellaneous thoughts that you needed to acknowledge and set aside.

Step 6: Once you have all the post-its in groups, name each group and place the title on the wall with the applicable group. Sometimes a different color post-it or pen is useful to distinguish the title from the individual post-it thoughts.

Step 7: Step back again, and see if there are any ideas that you’d like to move to a different group. If more thoughts come to mind, write them down and add them to the mix.

Depending on the approach you take with grouping your thoughts, you may end up with an outline for your workshop or a timeline for getting the work done or…

You’ll feel clearer. The process of “Operation Brain Dump” is an excellent tool for clearing your mind and gaining focus. Over the years, I’ve shared this technique with many clients to help them with everything from brainstorming website content and writing a proposal to determining next steps on a business issue and planning a wedding. Their experiences have been expressed as: “I feel like a load has been lifted from my shoulders” and “My head doesn’t feel like mush anymore” and “Now I know exactly what I need to do.”

In my own experience, I’ve found that, once I completed these steps, I was free to create from a de-cluttered mind and not be afraid I’d miss an important point I wanted to remember. I was able to move my thoughts from the wall to my computer, flesh out my presentation, and develop PowerPoint slides from the outline that had evolved through this process.

Warning: Be prepared for family or friends who visit while you’re working on “Operation Brain Dump.” They may think you’ve really lost it and are now taking up weird decorating techniques!
